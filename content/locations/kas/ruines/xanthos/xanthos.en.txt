Xanthos — The Tragic Capital of Freedom-Loving Lycia
Ancient Xanthos, located on a hill overlooking the fertile valley of the Esen River, was the capital and most powerful city of the Lycian League. Its history is not only a tale of prosperity and culture, but also a tragic saga of incredible love for freedom, which twice led the city to complete self-destruction.

Main Feature: Unique Tomb Monuments and Heroic History
Story of freedom: Xanthos is famous for two acts of mass suicide. In 545 BC, when the city was besieged by the Persians, the citizens of Xanthos, realizing the inevitability of defeat, gathered their wives, children, and all valuables in the acropolis, set it on fire, and launched a final deadly attack on the enemy. Almost the same was repeated in 42 BC during the siege of the city by the Roman troops of Brutus. This desperate devotion to freedom has forever written the name of Xanthos into the annals of history.

Unique funerary architecture: Xanthos is a true treasure trove of Lycian funerary art. Here you can see all its types: from simple rock-cut tombs to unique monuments. The most famous of them — the Harpy Monument and the Nereid Monument — were taken away in the 19th century and are now gems of the British Museum in London. However, their bases and other impressive sarcophagi and pillar tombs remain on site.

Stroll through the Capital: What to See?
Roman Theater: A well-preserved theater that stands on the site of an older Lycian one. Right behind its stage rise the famous tombs.

Lycian Acropolis: The site where the most important buildings once stood and where the city's inhabitants met their tragic fate.

Agora: The market square of the Roman period.

Xanthian Obelisk (Stele): A unique monument covered with the longest known inscription in the Lycian language, which has not yet been fully deciphered.

Byzantine Basilica: Ruins of a large early Christian church with beautiful mosaic floors.

Pros and Cons
Pros:

Immense historical significance as the capital of an entire civilization.
Unique examples of Lycian funerary architecture.
A dramatic and moving history.
UNESCO World Heritage status.

Cons:

The most famous monuments (Nereid and Harpy) were moved to London.
Requires a good imagination to picture the city's former grandeur from its ruins.
It can be very hot in summer.

Who Will Find Xanthos Interesting?
Xanthos is a place for those who want to delve deeper into the history of Lycia. Visiting Xanthos, especially in conjunction with Letoon, gives a complete picture of the political, religious, and cultural life of this unique civilization. It is not just about exploring ruins, but an opportunity to walk on land that witnessed incredible courage and tragedy, and to touch the history of a people for whom freedom was dearer than life.
